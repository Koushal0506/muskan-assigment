LED Characteristics Experiment

Studying the voltage-current characteristics of a Light Emitting Diode

Start Experiment Learn Theory

Objective

To study the voltage-current characteristics of a Light Emitting Diode (LED) and observe its behavior under forward bias conditions.

Theory

The light emitting diode (LED) is a solid state source. LEDs have replaced incandescent lamps in many applications because they have the following advantages:

  • Low voltage operation
  • Long life (more than two years)
  • Fast ON-OFF switching (nanoseconds)

In a forward biased rectifier diode, free electrons and holes recombine at a junction. When a free electron falls into a hole, it drops from a higher energy level to lower energy level. As the electron falls it radiates energy in the form of heat and light. Because silicon is opaque (not transparent), none of the light escapes to the environment.

An LED is different - semi-transparent materials are used instead of silicon. In forward-biased LED, heat and light are again radiated when free electrons and holes recombine at the junction. Because the material is semi-transparent, some of the light escapes to the surroundings.

By using elements like gallium, arsenic and phosphorus, manufacturers can produce LEDs that radiate red, green, yellow, amber or infrared (invisible) light. LEDs that produce visible radiations are used in instrument displays like calculators, digital clocks, etc. The infrared LED finds applications in burglar-alarm systems and other areas requiring invisible radiations.

LEDs have a typical voltage drop from 1.5V to 2.5V for currents between 10mA to 50mA. Incidentally, LED has low reverse voltage rating of 3V. This means accidentally applying a reverse voltage greater than 3V may destroy or damage the LED characteristics.

Procedure

  1. Connect the circuit as shown in the circuit diagram through patch chords.
  2. Switch ON the instrument using ON/OFF toggle switch provided on the front panel.
  3. Vary the input voltage in small steps and note down the voltage and current readings.
  4. Record at least 10 readings of voltage and corresponding current.
  5. Plot a graph of LED current versus LED voltage.
  6. Observe the characteristics curve and note the threshold voltage where current starts increasing rapidly.

Precautions

  • All connections should be tight and secure.
  • Readings should be noted very carefully.
  • Do not exceed the maximum current rating of the LED.
  • Increase the voltage gradually to avoid damaging the LED.
  • Ensure proper polarity when connecting the LED.

Viva Questions

A Light Emitting Diode (LED) is a semiconductor device that emits light when an electric current passes through it. It works on the principle of electroluminescence where electron-hole recombination releases energy in the form of photons.

An LED is formed by joining p-type and n-type semiconductor materials. When forward biased, electrons from the n-side and holes from the p-side recombine at the junction, releasing energy in the form of light.

Different colored LEDs are made using different semiconductor materials with varying band gaps. The color of light emitted depends on the energy band gap of the semiconductor material used in the LED.

Key differences:

  • LED emits light when forward biased, while PN diode doesn't
  • LED uses direct bandgap semiconductors, PN diode can use indirect bandgap
  • LED has transparent packaging to let light escape
  • LED has higher forward voltage drop than silicon PN diode
